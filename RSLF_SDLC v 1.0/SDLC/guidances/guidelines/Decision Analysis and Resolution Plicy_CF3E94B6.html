Decision Analysis and Resolution Policy

　

Formal decision-making has to be followed in the major project decision that impact project overall success. Decisions fall under two main categories:

　

A) Management Decisions:

　

　　　　　　　 Decisions related to the overall project approach such as product type (Website, Web Service, Desktop Application ), main external integrations with other systems. Decisions can also cover business or development standards, vendor or supplier selections

　

　　　　　　　 Formal decisions are encouraged whenever there is possible impact on project success and customer satisfaction. The PM decides when to apply formal decision-making.

　

　　　　　　　 Some of the important criteria in evaluating management decision include but not limited to:

　



  • Customer Satisfaction　(high)




  • Cost (development, operation) (medium)




  • Time (medium)




  • Resource (availability, knowledge, experience) (medium)


　B) Technical Decision:

　

　　　　　　　 Decisions taken by the team leads and system architects related to the architecture and high-level design of the application. This category includes a wide range of decisions that include selection of technologies, tools,　third party components, reuse, design options.

　

　　　　　　　　Formal decisions are obligatory for architectural decisions and main development technology whenever more than one option is available and not specified in the requirements. Formal decisions are optional for other　design decisions.

　

　　　　　　　 Some of the important criteria in evaluating technical decision include but not limited to:

　



  • Availability (high)




  • Usability (high)




  • Performance (medium)




  • Scalability (medium)




  • Maintainability (low)




  • Reusability (low)


　　　　　　　　 The exact criteria has to be agreed between the team lead and system architect on a case by case basis.　

　

Some decisions might have both management and technical dimensions and require a combination of criteria. The decison maker(s) decide on the eaxct weight (based on the above initial weights) and comparison/selection method to be applied in each decison　on a case-by-case basis.

　

The following is the general approach to be followed in formal decision-making ( meeting is the evaluation method in all types ) :

　

1- Identify alternative solutions to be chosen from.

2- Define the applicable criteria and weight based on the subject decision and project context and get team consensus (in case of group decision).

3- Evaluate alternative solutions　based on criteria.　

4- Select the solution that achieves the highest match to the criteria.

5- If more than one decision are very close, revise the criteria and re-evaluate.

6- Document the participants and details of the meeting in an MoM.

7- Share the results of the decision making with the senior management.

3.1       Grid Analysis - Making a Choice Where Many Factors Must Be Balanced

Grid Analysis is a useful technique for making a decision. It is most effective where you have a number of good alternatives and many factors to take into account.

3.1.1        How to use the technique

1.     Work out the relative importance of the factors in your decision. Show these as numbers and use them to weight your preferences by the importance of the factor. These values may be obvious. If they are not, then use a technique such as Paired Comparison Analysis to estimate them.

2.     Work your way across the table, scoring each option for each of the important factors in your decision. Score each option from 0 (poor) to 3 (very good). Note that you do not have to give a different score for each option - if none of them are good for a particular factor in your decision, then all options should have a score of 0.

3.     Now multiply each of your scores by the values for your relative importance. This will give them the correct overall weight in your decision.

4.     Finally add up these weighted scores for your options. The option that scores the highest wins!

3.1.3        Key Points

Grid Analysis helps you to decide between several options, while taking many different factors into account.

To use the technique, lay out your options as rows on a table. Set up the columns to show your factors. Allocate weights to show the importance of each of these factors.

Score each choice for each factor using numbers from 0 (poor) to 3 (very good). Multiply each score by the weight of the factor, to show its contribution to the overall selection.

Finally add up the total scores for each option. Select the highest scoring option.
