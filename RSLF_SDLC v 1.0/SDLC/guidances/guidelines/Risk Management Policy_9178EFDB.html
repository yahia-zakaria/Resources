Guideline: Risk Management Policy
The purpose of the Risk Management Policy is to ensure that all the current and future software development risk exposures are identified, assessed, quantified, appropriately mitigated and managed.

Main Description

Overview

This policy establishes a framework for the risk management process and to ensure organization wide implementation.

 

 

Risk Management Policy

In order to fulfill the objectives of this policy and lay a strong foundation for the development of an integrated risk management framework, the policy outlines the following guiding principles of Risk Management:

 

Principles of Risk Management

1.     Project Manager should visit the portal during the planning and it needs to be revisited frequently for any updates in order to identifying the possible project-specific risks.

2.       All software project team members of shall be made aware of risks in their respective domains and their mitigation.

 

3.      The occurrence, progress and status of all risks will be promptly reported and appropriate actions be taken thereof.

4.      Process Engineer Collect the common risks from the project every 3 months and add this risks to portal as part of organizational learning

5.      Process Engineer can delete on portal after Management approval.

 

Risk Management Policy Statement

 

1.       To ensure protection shareholder value through the establishment of an integrated Risk Management Framework for identifying, assessing, mitigating, monitoring, evaluating and reporting of all risks.

 

2.       To provide clear and strong basis for informed decision making at all levels of software development related departments and organization units.

 

Risk Management Activities

This section describes the risk management activities to be performed during the project and including the following:

 

[A] Identify and Categorize Potential Risks

When the software project manager identifies risks, the project team considers “what can go wrong”. At the broadest level, everything can go wrong.

Risk Identification determines which risks might affect the project and documents their characteristics.  Risk Identification is therefore similar to Cause / Effect Analysis. Categorizing the Risk is based on the source of the main cause.  This is the Risk's "Category" and should be one of the following standardized sources:

 

 

 

1.        Organization Risks

-          Is there sufficient commitment to this project (including management, testers, QA, and other external but involved parties)?

-          Is this the largest project has ever attempted?

-          Is there a well-defined requirements capture and management?

2.      Budget Risks

-          Is the budget in place to complete project?

-          Are there budget limitations such that the system must be delivered at a fixed cost or be subject to cancellation?

-          Are cost estimates accurate?

3.      People Risks

-          Are enough people available?

-          Do they have appropriate skills and experience?

-          Have they worked together before?

-          Do they believe the project can succeed?

-          Are user representatives available for reviews?

-          Are domain experts available? 

4.      Schedule Risks

-          Is the schedule realistic?

-          Can functionality be scope-managed to meet schedules?

-          How critical is the delivery date?

-          Is there time to "do it right"?

5.      Scope Risks

-          Can success be measured?

-          Is there agreement on how to measure success?

-          Are the requirements fairly stable and well understood?

-          Is the project scope firm or does the scope keeps expanding?

-          Are the project development time scales short and inflexible?

6.      Technological Risks

-          Has the technology been proven?

-          Are reuse objectives reasonable?

-          Are there unusual or challenging technical requirements that require the project team to tackle problems with which they are unfamiliar?

-          Is success dependent on new or untried products, services or technologies, new or unproven hardware, software, or techniques?

-          Are there external dependencies on interfaces to other systems, including those outside the enterprise?  Do the required interfaces exist or must they be created?

-          Are there availability and security requirements which are extremely inflexible (for example, "the system must never fail")?

-          Are the users of the system inexperienced with the type of system being developed?

-          Is there increased risk due to the size or complexity of the application or the newness of the technology?

-          Is it possible to design, implement, and run this system? Some systems are just too huge or complex to ever work properly.

-       PSs not working

7.       Dependency Risks

-          Does the project depend on other (parallel) development projects?

-          Is success dependent on off the shelf products or externally-developed components?

-          Is success dependent on the successful integration of development tools (design tools, compilers, and so on), implementation technologies (operating systems, databases, inter-process communication mechanisms, and so on)?

-          Do you have a back-up plan for delivering the project without these technologies?

1-Critical, 2-High, 3-Medium, or 4-Low. Indication of the severity value can be guided as follows:

4-Low

A Mitigation action is defined as the actions taken to ensure that the severity of the risk is reduced or the probability is reduced.

For Eg: Confirming with the customer about the availability and setup of the network for the deployment stage during design phase and initiating actions to ensure items are in place well before the deployment.

A Contingency action is an action that is invoked after the risk has actually materialized.

 

 

Examples:

-          To reduce the risk that products X and Y cannot be integrated, a prototype will be built to investigate the difficulty of integration. The following features (enumerate in a list) will be tested to ensure the integration is successful.

-          To reduce the risk that Database A will not perform adequately, it will be benchmarked using a suite of tests which model the workload of the target application.

-          To reduce the risk that test tool Z will not be able to effectively regression test the application, we will acquire and use it during the upcoming iteration.

 

The result of these actions should be to reduce the probability that certain risks will occur, perhaps to near zero. In cases where the risk is confirmed, the risk is responded to with a contingency plan.

 

Risk mitigation (i.e., handling) techniques include:

 

[1] Risk Avoidance

Risk avoidance is concerned with changing the situation to reduce the probability of the potential problem to an acceptable level.

 

[2] Risk transfer

Risk transfer involves reallocating the requirements that created the risk factor to another system component or another organizational unit that can better handle the risk factor.

 

[3] Immediate action

Immediate actions are risk mitigation activities that are undertaken now to reduce the probability and/or potential impact of a potential problem (i.e., a risk category) that may later become a real problem. Immediate actions are specified in immediate action plans.

 

 [4] Risk Acceptance

Acceptance involves acknowledging the risk source but taking no action at the present time other than placing the risk on a watch (risk) list. Although risk acceptance does not result in a specific mitigation activity, each risk source on the watch (risk) list is frequently re-examined on a periodic basis to determine if the level of probability, severity, or time frame has become prominent enough to warrant additional mitigation activities (e.g., avoidance, transfer or immediate action).

 

Mitigation planning is identified by describing two attributes:

1- Mitigation Plan:

Identifies the conditions or events that determine whether a risk should be mitigated using specific mitigation strategy.

 

2- Mitigation Triggers:

Specifies the conditions under which the risk mitigation strategy will be initiated.

 

[D] Identify Contingency Strategies

[E] Reviewing and Updating Risks  

All types of risks must be tracked, reviewed and updated with new action items weekly with the issuance of status reports. This must be inserted in the project schedule as recurring task.

 

Risk Workflow

 

Typical workflow progression:

 

-          A Project Manager creates a risk in the Proposed state with the default reason, New.

-          A Project Manager changes the state of the risk from Proposed to Active with the default reason, Accepted.

-          A Project Manager changes the state of the risk from Active to Resolved after the team completes all mitigation actions.

-          A Project Manager changes the state of the risk from Resolved to Closed when the team validates the risk as having been mitigated.
